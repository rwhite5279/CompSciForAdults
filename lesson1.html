    • Discussion Questions

      Computer Science for All?

      Pick one or more of the articles above to read.

      When you're finished, discuss the various perspectives in the articles, perhaps using some of these questions as a starting point for your conversation.

      1. A need to fill computing-related job openings is commonly given as one reason for increasing the availability of Computer Science education in schools. In the CSforALL article, what are some additional reasons cited? Do you agree with these reasons?
      2. The CSforALL article compares the desire to increase CS education to a modern day Space Race, the Cold War-era competition with Russia that culminated with a manned landing on the moon. How appropriate is this comparison?
      3. Anil Dash describes America's relationship with technology as a "secular religion." Do you agree with that statement? Cite evidence to support or refute that idea.
      4. Dash has concerns about technology companies. Do you agree with his criticisms?
      5. Ultimately, does Dash support the idea of CS for all? Why?
      6. The Des Moines Register article reports that Iowa is considering joining over twenty other states that require Computer Science coursework for graduation. What are some of the issues Iowa is facing around this legislation?
      7. The Education Week article focuses on efforts in Illinois to provide a CS education for its students. Pick one of the concerns here and consider your stance on the topic:
        1. "There are fears about quality: As the field attracts money and attention, schools are being flooded with sales pitches for platforms, devices, and games claiming to make coding instruction easy."
        2. "There are fears about the future: Companies may be looking for hard-core Java developers right now, but advances in both hardware and artificial intelligence could render such skills irrelevant by the time today's 6th graders hit the job market."
        3. "There are fears that some 'Computer Science for All' proponents don't understand or appreciate the context of public education. 'Developing a workforce that knows how to use a particular software or [programming] language is not what high school is for,' said Cuny of the National Science Foundation."
